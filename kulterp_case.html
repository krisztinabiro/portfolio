KultERP

A DMS for cultural institutions

I designed this application during my internship at SmartX Solutions, a Hungarian software company, where I took full responsibility for the entire UX and UI design. Throughout the project, I was supervised by a mentor who provided invaluable guidance and support as I navigated various design challenges. While the design presented below highlights my individual contributions, it’s important to acknowledge that the overall development process was a collaborative effort involving a dedicated team of developers and analysts.

This software was developed and designed in Hungarian, specifically tailored for a unique client, with the potential for customization to meet the needs of various institutions in the future.

Problem 📌

A new legislation has made it mandatory for cultural institutions, such as theaters, to utilize complex document management systems.

The main challenge of this project was to create a user-friendly desktop application for a target audience unfamiliar with complex digital solutions, all while adhering to strict legal requirements. Users were primarily motivated to adopt the new system due to external pressures and were satisfied with their previous methods. Thus, we aimed to develop a solution that not only met the new regulations but also showed users that the digital transition could be seamless and beneficial.


Process 🔄


Studying the legislation

Initially, we outlined the basic functionality of the application. As it needed approval from the authorities, we had to adhere to the rules specified in recent legislation. This required us to carefully examine the relevant laws and prepare the app's specification accordingly.
Throughout this phase, I worked closely with our business analyst and legal consultant to delineate the application's features. Additionally, I played a significant role in preparing the requirements specification.


Understanding our users

Once we understood the legal requirements for the application, we focused on identifying the specific needs of our target users in their daily workflows. Since we collaborated with the Budapest Operetta Theater, we had a well-defined user group, including accountants, secretaries, and other administrative staff members. This clarity allowed us to tailor our design to meet their unique requirements effectively.

We used the following methods as part of the discovery period of our process:

Task analysis
We employed this method because the product targeted specific work-related tasks that were initially unfamiliar to us. It was crucial for us to understand the frequently performed tasks and their steps, as we needed to familiarize ourselves with the intricacies of the field to create an effective solution.

Contextual inquiry
We believed this method would yield deeper insights than traditional interviews. By observing three key users performing their most frequent tasks with their current methods, we identified which workarounds would be most beneficial. This approach was invaluable in pinpointing areas where we could offer a better digital solution compared to their existing semi-digital processes, which primarily involved Excel spreadsheets and physical document storage. For example, we found that users could greatly benefit from a more efficient document search system that would allow them to link existing documents to newly created ones.


Preparing sketches

I have begun the ideation process by creating sketches. These sketches include low-fidelity versions of the screens, which enable me to explore various layouts and user flows.


User testing

We conducted four rounds of user testing with five key users to ensure the app meets their needs and facilitates a seamless transition from their current system.
Fortunately, we had three key users from the target group working closely with us, allowing us to discuss initial sketches and iterate towards higher-fidelity prototypes while testing with them along the way.
Most of the time, we employed a talk-aloud method for testing, but we also held a brainstorming session aimed at outlining additional functions to simplify the most frequent task of adding a new document to the repository.

The continuous user testing allowed me to reconsider my design choices several times, making the design process iterative, resulting in a 28% increase in task success rate.

A few key insights:

  • Initially, I employed a nearly white background for the screens. However, after receiving feedback from prospective users who found it too bright and obtrusive, I made the decision to switch to an ivory background.

  • At first, I implemented multilevel dropdown menus. However, based on the feedback received during testing, it became apparent that they contributed to more complex and extended user journeys. Additionally, since a majority of our prospective users are administrators utilizing a limited number of functions, I made the decision to simplify the user journeys. This involved linking each menu item directly to a frequently used function.


Solution 💡

Through close collaboration with our clients and a careful study of the legislation, we aimed to design a user-friendly interface featuring meaningful icons to facilitate easy navigation. We also sought to minimize the learning curve by incorporating elements from the existing document management methods used by the theater.

UI decisions


  • Skeuomorphic buttons

    • I chose to use skeuomorphic buttons paired with a paper-like background.
    This approach aims to enhance the interface's intuitiveness, making the digital environment feel more familiar for users who have primarily worked with physical documents in the past.


  • Accordion

    • User testing revealed that users complete forms more efficiently when all fields are visible at once without the need to scroll. This insight led to prioritizing the reduction of scrolling as a key design goal. To achieve this, I implemented accordion components on pages with extensive forms, particularly for sections that were auto-filled or optional. This approach helped streamline lengthy content, resulting in a cleaner and more user-friendly interface.


  • Clarifying icons

    • To enhance the recognizability of call-to-action and navigation elements, I've incorporated a variety of clarifying icons into my design.
    Given the software's intended purpose for everyday work use, I believe that embedding glyph icons within the input fields and accentuating icons in the menubar significantly eases perception, thereby enhancing the overall efficacy of the software.
